# Q1 Use Case Design: AI-Augmented Due Diligence at a Mid-Size Spanish Law Firm
 
## Context
 
**Industry:** Legal services, M&A and financial regulation  
**Company:** Iberia Legal Partners, SL - 150-person law firm in Madrid. 80 attorneys, 40 paralegals, 30 support staff. Specialises in corporate acquisitions, capital markets transactions, and CNMV regulatory compliance for financial institutions.
 
**Problem today:** Each M&A deal generates 500-2,000 pages of contracts, financial statements, and regulatory filings. Senior associates spend around 2.5 hours a day manually hunting for specific clauses: change-of-control provisions, indemnity caps, material adverse change definitions. Paralegals copy-paste from Word templates to produce first-draft NDAs and keep introducing version inconsistencies that reviewers have to catch. The compliance team manually monitors 12 regulatory RSS feeds and cross-references each new CNMV circular against 40 active client portfolios. That's about 4 hours per week per compliance officer, none of it billable.
 
The firm piloted ChatGPT Enterprise in Q1 2025 and then stopped. The Data Protection Officer flagged that confidential client M&A data was going to OpenAI's US infrastructure, which conflicts with GDPR Article 44 and the firm's client confidentiality obligations under Art. 21 of the Estatuto General de la Abogacía. The firm needs a GDPR-compliant, EU-resident alternative that can query internal document repositories without sending data outside.
 
---
 
## Personas
 
### Persona 1: Narcís Agustí, Senior Associate (8 years, M&A)
 
**Workflow:** Narcís manages due diligence on 3 concurrent acquisition targets at once. For each deal he reads 200-400 pages of target-company contracts and produces a clause-by-clause summary memo for the supervising partner before the weekly deal call.
 
**Pain points:** Ctrl+F is his main search tool. Missing a single change-of-control clause in a 600-page contract can expose the acquirer to unexpected third-party consent requirements post-closing, a career-defining mistake. Each memo takes about 6 hours right now, and there's no budget for extra paralegal support.
 
### Persona 2: Víctor Perez, Paralegal (3 years, transactional)
 
**Workflow:** Víctor prepares first drafts of NDAs, board resolutions, powers of attorney, and share purchase agreement schedules using templates from the firm's Notion workspace. Drafts go to the supervising associate before client delivery.
 
**Pain points:** The Notion templates were last updated in 2022. Víctor manually adapts boilerplate for each deal: party names, governing law clauses, deal-specific recitals. Reviewers end up correcting formatting inconsistencies and clause numbering errors instead of focusing on the actual substance. Average first-draft time: 45 minutes per document.
 
### Persona 3: Emma Solís, Compliance Officer
 
**Workflow:** Emma monitors CNMV circulars, ESMA guidelines, and Banco de España notifications for the firm's financial-sector clients. Each new publication gets cross-referenced against 40 active mandates before the client relationship partner hears about it.
 
**Pain points:** New guidance arrives as dense 80-page PDFs. Figuring out whether CNMV Circular 2/2025 affects any active client takes 45 minutes of reading and cross-referencing. Emma covers 3-5 new publications a week, roughly 4 hours of non-billable work.

## Journey: Step-by-Step Interaction with the Stack
 
### Journey A: Narcís's Due Diligence Review
 
**Step 1: Upload.** Narcís attaches 12 PDF contracts (680 pages total) via the LobeChat file attachment UI. Files land in MinIO bucket `lobe` under `s3://lobe/uploads/<user-id>/<deal-id>/` via the **minio-mcp** tool, which issues a presigned PUT URL to the browser. MinIO is accessed through Caddy at `https://minio.54-170-130-123.sslip.io`.
 
**Step 2: Indexing.** LobeChat triggers the RAG pipeline: documents split into 512-token chunks with 64-token overlap, embedded using OpenRouter's `openai/text-embedding-3-small` ($0.02/MTok, 1536 dimensions), vectors stored in Qdrant on the same EC2 instance. Each chunk carries the source filename and page number as metadata.
 
**Step 3: Query.** Narcís types: *"List every change-of-control clause across all uploaded contracts. For each clause, state which party must consent and the applicable notice period."*
 
**Step 4: Retrieval.** Qdrant runs a cosine similarity search over 12,000 stored vectors and returns the top 15 relevant chunks. Those get re-ranked by MMR (Maximum Marginal Relevance) to reduce redundancy across documents.
 
**Step 5: Inference.** The retrieved chunks (~28,000 tokens of context) plus the query go to `anthropic/claude-3-5-sonnet` via OpenRouter (200k context window). The model returns a structured table: contract name, clause reference, consenting party, notice period, and a risk flag where consent is irrevocable. Streaming over SSE takes about 22 seconds.
 
**Step 6: MCP cross-check.** Narcís follows up: *"Are there any clauses referencing a notice period shorter than 10 days?"* The **filesystem MCP tool** (via MCPHub) runs `grep -ri "days" /app/uploads/<deal-id>/` on the server as a raw-text ground-truth check, specifically to catch anything the model might miss.
 
**Step 7: Output.** Narcís downloads a formatted Markdown summary via a MinIO presigned URL and drops it into Word. Partner review: 15 minutes. Total attorney time down from 6 hours to 45 minutes.
 
**Data flow:** Browser > HTTPS (Caddy/443) > LobeChat > MinIO (file store) > Qdrant (vector retrieval) > OpenRouter API (inference) > SSE stream back to browser.

### Journey B: Víctor's NDA Drafting
 
**Step 1.** Víctor types: *"Draft an NDA for Project Omega between Vendedor S.A. (disclosing party) and Comprador Corp (receiving party), governed by Spanish law, valid for 24 months, using our standard bilateral template."*
 
**Step 2.** The **notion-mcp** tool pulls the current bilateral NDA template from the firm's Notion database (`/Templates/NDA Bilateral v2024-Q4`) via the Notion API. The template includes versioning metadata showing it was last reviewed by a partner in December 2024.
 
**Step 3.** LobeChat routes to `anthropic/claude-3-haiku` ($0.25/$1.25 per MTok in/out). First-draft NDA generation doesn't need Sonnet's full reasoning. The model substitutes all template variables, produces a properly numbered 8-clause NDA, and flags three fields that still need manual input: governing court jurisdiction, definition of "Confidential Information", and signature blocks.
 
**Step 4.** Víctor gets a fully formatted Markdown draft in 9 seconds, pastes it into Word, fills the three flagged fields, and submits for associate review. Total time: 8 minutes vs. 45 previously.
 
---
 
### Journey C: Emma's Regulatory Monitoring
 
**Step 1.** Emma uploads the PDF of CNMV Circular 3/2025.
 
**Step 2.** She asks: *"Does this circular impose new reporting obligations on investment firms? Cross-reference against our active capital markets mandates."*
 
**Step 3.** Qdrant retrieves the top 10 most relevant chunks from the new circular alongside mandate summaries from 40 active clients (previously indexed from the CRM export stored in PostgreSQL).
 
**Step 4.** The **aws-resources-operations MCP tool** checks an S3 bucket (`s3://firm-regulatory-archive/cnmv/`) where prior CNMV PDFs are stored, verifying whether Circular 3/2025 explicitly supersedes any earlier circular already in Emma's client workflows.
 
**Step 5.** `anthropic/claude-3-5-sonnet` cross-references the new obligations against each client's activity profile and returns a flagged list: 3 clients have active investment advisory mandates that now require quarterly reporting disclosures under the new circular, with specific paragraph references.
 
**Step 6.** Emma drafts a client alert memo in 18 minutes, down from 45 minutes of manual reading.

## Why This Stack vs. ChatGPT Enterprise / Microsoft Copilot
 
### Reason 1: GDPR and Professional Secrecy
 
Spanish law imposes strict professional secrecy obligations on attorneys under Art. 21 of the Estatuto General de la Abogacía. GDPR Article 44 prohibits transferring personal data outside the EU/EEA without adequate safeguards. ChatGPT Enterprise routes inference to OpenAI's US infrastructure. Even with Standard Contractual Clauses, the firm's DPO and its clients' internal legal teams rejected this arrangement because M&A counterparty identities and deal terms are personal data under GDPR.
 
This stack keeps everything on EC2 in AWS eu-west-1 (Ireland). The only outbound call is OpenRouter for inference, and even that can be cut by enabling the vLLM service already in `docker-compose.yml` (gated behind the `gpu` profile) on a GPU instance for the most sensitive matters. That option simply doesn't exist with any SaaS AI product.
 
### Reason 2: Internal System Integration Without Data Externalisation
 
ChatGPT and Copilot can call third-party connectors, but each one routes data through the vendor's own infrastructure: Microsoft Graph API, OpenAI Actions API. Connecting the firm's Notion workspace, S3 regulatory archive, and local deal files to those platforms would introduce additional data controllers, each requiring a separate DPA and client notification.
 
MCPHub in this stack exposes **notion-mcp**, **filesystem**, **minio-mcp**, and **aws-resources-operations** as tools callable from within the firm's AWS VPC. No data leaves the VPC during tool invocation. That's the real difference: the AI acts on internal systems with no external data pathway.
 
### Reason 3: Per-Task Model Routing and Actual Cost Control
 
ChatGPT Enterprise charges $60/user/month for GPT-4o regardless of whether you're generating an NDA draft or doing deep clause analysis. This stack routes tasks to cost-appropriate models: embeddings use `text-embedding-3-small` ($0.02/MTok), first drafts use `claude-3-haiku` ($0.25/$1.25 per MTok in/out), and high-stakes analysis uses `claude-3-5-sonnet` ($3/$15 per MTok in/out). At 80 attorneys sending 20 messages per day with a 70/30 Haiku/Sonnet split, token cost drops roughly 68% compared to always using Sonnet.
 
At 100 monthly active users the full stack runs around $525/month, or $5.25/user/month. That's a 91% cost reduction vs. ChatGPT Enterprise for the same workflows.
